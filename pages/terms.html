BATTERY TERMINOLOGY

The conventional automotive battery is made to be a wet-cell and is rechargeable. All batteries, regardless of the type of car, have the same function: they supply electricity to all of the electrical parts of the vehicle. The internal chemical engines of your car are started when you turn on the battery, which provides electric current. Furthermore, the battery is necessary for the proper operation of other electronic components in the car, such as the lights, radio, wipers, etc.

A battery box or case usually encloses a car battery. On the other hand, the car battery's internal parts are made of various materials. The necessary automobile battery components are listed below.

Battery Acid

The acid is a 36-40 percent sulfuric acid and water solution. The combination that is left behind functions as the electrolyte, causing the chemical reaction that generates the electric current that drives the car. Since the presence of impurities may reduce its effectiveness for automobile power generation, it is frequently quite pure. When the car is ignited, the acid and water react, creating the appropriate voltage required to start the car. If the battery is weak, there won't be enough voltage to run the car engine.

Battery Terminal

Another name for it is battery bushing. The point of connection between the battery and the electrical systems of the car is known as the battery terminal. It has two terminals, much like any other electrical device: the positive and negative straps of the battery end cells.

Battery Case

Batteries are housed in boxes in both gasoline-powered and electric cars. The car battery's components and contents are shielded by the case. Polypropylene resins are frequently used in the design of cast-on straps for fuel batteries. The battery enclosure box in electric vehicles is typically made of aluminium alloys. The battery case prolongs the battery's life by protecting its contents and reducing impact forces on the battery.

Battery Plates

Positive and negative plates make up a standard automobile battery. Both plates have a metal grid with a spongy lead at the negative end and lead dioxide substance at the positive end. A cast-on strap connects each part to the battery cells on top of each plate.

Battery Seperator

The separator, as its name suggests, makes a separation between the positive and negative plates. Moreover, polyethylene or other plastic polymers are frequently used to make it.

Battery Polarity

Another name for it is battery bushing. The point of connection between the battery and the electrical systems of the car is known as the battery terminal. It has two terminals, much like any other electrical device: the positive and negative straps of the battery end cells.

Electrolyte

An acidic water mixture that transfers charge between the positive and negative plates by means of ions, or charged particles in the electrolyte solution. It provides sulphate ions so that they can react with the plates' active ingredient.
